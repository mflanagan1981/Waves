2026 All-Star Cut Proposal · Old Dominion Swim League

How might we set All-Star cuts that fill every event with the right-sized field?

A cut has to do two things at once: feel fair, and leave a roughly even number of swimmers in each event. Eyeballing it hasn’t worked — over the last two seasons, events ran from 45 swimmers down to 10.

  • Two patterns hold steady season to season: how much each age group improves after Meet 2, and what share of its qualifiers actually swim.
  • Pin those down and all 52 cut lines follow from a single choice — the field size you want per event.
Recommended target ~22 swimmers / event · 18–24 just right

This brief recommends All-Star fields of 18 to 24 swimmers per event — though the tool will set cuts for whatever size ODSL deems appropriate. A target of 22 entrants gives any event the best odds of landing in that window: run on the 2025 season, it puts about 41 of 52 events there.

NoteTwo checks before adopting. Any proposed cut that moves more than ±5% from the 2025 cut should be reviewed by hand — a swing that large usually flags a thin or volatile event rather than a real shift in the field. And because these cuts are set from two meets, folding in Meet 3 rankings would tighten the projection further.

The shift

A cut shouldn’t be carried over from last year’s time — the field of swimmers changes every season. What stays stable is how a field improves and what share of it turns out. So read this year’s first two meets, project them forward on that historical curve, weight by who actually swims, and you set how many swimmers per event you want.

The problem

How often the cuts hit the target

Held to an 18-to-24 standard, most events miss — and the bigger problem is fields that run too small, not too large. Hitting the window by hand, event by event, is hard. A target-based rule makes the field size a deliberate choice instead.

What the data shows

Two patterns hold steady, year to year

The whole method rests on two things the 2024 and 2025 seasons agree on. Neither is a guess — both repeat across years, and both move predictably with age.

1 · How much each group improves

From the cut-setting meet to Divisionals every group keeps dropping time — the youngest fastest, the oldest barely. Boys edge girls by about a point. So the improvement multiplier is graded by age and gender, not blanket.

2 · How many qualifiers actually swim

Making the cut isn’t the same as swimming the event. Some qualifiers skip All-Stars; others come but spend their three event slots elsewhere. The field that shows up is always smaller than the qualifier list — and the gap widens with age.

Put together, that is the entire model: pick the field size you want, let the improvement curve and the turnout rate convert it, and the cut for every event falls out.

What this does and doesn’t do

A target sets the center, not the spread

Even with a target, field sizes still vary by about ±3 swimmers — from projection error and how tightly swimmers cluster near the cut — and the projection itself misses by 4–5% on the noisiest events. What the target does is real: it pulls the spread down from roughly ±6 to ±3 and lands about 41 of 52 events in the 18–24 window, up from 22 under the 2025 cuts. It will not make every event identical, and it does not replace judgment on the youngest, most volatile groups.

The youngest brackets are volatile

6&Under and 8&Under fields are small, some barely reach 24 swimmers early, and their times drop the fastest. Their factors carry the most uncertainty — sanity-check against the prior year before adopting.

How these cuts are set

These cuts read each event’s field after two meets and project it to Divisionals on the age-graded improvement curve. The method is what’s being proposed; the numbers move only if late or corrected results post.

The 2024 cut is reconstructed

We never had the published 2024 standards, so 2024 figures are inferred from the slowest qualifier who swam each event — a proxy, not an official cut. The 2025 analysis, built on real published cuts, is the firmer ground.
